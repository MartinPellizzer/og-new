**Paragraph 1: Introduction**
Autism is a neurodevelopmental disorder characterized by difficulties in social communication and restricted repetitive behaviors. Environmental factors, including air pollution and maternal nutrient intake during pregnancy, have been linked to autism risk. This study aimed to examine the joint associations between prenatal air pollutant exposure and nutrient intake with children's autism-related traits scores.

**Paragraph 2: Methods**
The study used data from the Early Autism Risk Longitudinal Investigation (EARLI) cohort, which followed pregnant mothers who previously had a child with autism. The authors used Bayesian kernel machine regression (BKMR) models to examine associations between prenatal air pollutant exposure and nutrient intake with children's autism-related traits scores, measured by the Social Responsiveness Scale (SRS) at 36 months of age.

**Paragraph 3: Results**
The analysis included 126 participants from the EARLI cohort. The authors found little evidence of individual or joint associations between air pollution exposures and nutrient intake with SRS scores. Traditional regression models also did not provide significant findings for bivariate interactions and effect modification.

**Paragraph 4: Discussion**
Despite the lack of significant findings, this study highlights the importance of examining the interplay between environmental factors and maternal nutrient intake during pregnancy on neurodevelopmental outcomes. The use of BKMR models allowed for the examination of complex associations between multiple exposures and outcomes. Future studies should explore these relationships in larger and more diverse samples to better understand the potential moderators of air pollution exposure on autism risk.

**Paragraph 5: Conclusion**
In conclusion, this study did not find evidence of joint associations between prenatal air pollutant exposure and nutrient intake with children's autism-related traits scores. However, the use of BKMR models provides a promising approach for examining complex relationships between environmental exposures and neurodevelopmental outcomes. Further research is needed to elucidate the potential role of maternal nutrition in moderating the association between air pollution and autism risk.
