**Paragraph 1: Introduction**
The study aimed to investigate the short and mid-term impact of ozone (O3) on myocardial infarction (MI) incidence in Poland. Previous research has shown that air pollution is a significant risk factor for cardiovascular disease, but there is limited data on the specific effects of O3. This study built upon previous work by examining the relationship between O3 concentrations and MI incidence in rural and urban areas of Poland.

**Paragraph 2: Methods**
The study used a retrospective cohort design to analyze data from over 100,000 individuals in Poland. Participants were divided into two groups based on their residential location (rural or urban) and exposure to O3 was measured using satellite imagery and ground-level monitoring stations. The relationship between O3 concentrations and MI incidence was examined using logistic regression analysis.

**Paragraph 3: Results**
The study found that the median concentration of O3 was 47.9 μg/m3, with significant seasonal and geographical variability. Exposure to O3 increased the risk of ST-elevation MI (STEMI) incidence on the day of exposure, especially in rural areas (RR 1.009, 95% CI 1.002–1.016). In contrast, there was no significant effect of O3 on non-ST-elevation MI (NSTEMI) incidence.

**Paragraph 4: Discussion**
The study's findings support previous research suggesting that O3 is a significant risk factor for cardiovascular disease. The results also highlight the importance of considering rural-urban differences in air pollution exposure when evaluating the effects of O3 on MI incidence. Furthermore, the study underscores the need to develop more effective mitigation strategies to reduce O3 concentrations, particularly in rural areas.

**Paragraph 5: Conclusions**
In conclusion, this study demonstrates that short and mid-term exposure to O3 is associated with an increased risk of STEMI incidence in Poland, especially in rural areas. The findings have significant implications for public health policy and suggest the need for further research on the effects of air pollution on cardiovascular disease. By integrating environmental risk factors into established risk assessment scales, we can better understand and mitigate the impact of O3 on MI incidence.
