The depletion of the ozone layer, a vital shield protecting the Earth from harmful ultraviolet (UV) radiation, has become a global environmental concern. Ozone-depleting substances (ODS), such as chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs) and hydrochlorofluorocarbons (HCFCs), have been causing harm to the ozone layer for decades. The Kigali Amendment to the Montreal Protocol, which focuses on phasing down HFCs, is a significant milestone in global efforts to control ODS. China stands out as a major source of contraband ODS, with other countries such as Bulgaria, Lithuania, Poland, and France reporting numerous cases. Analyzing these case instances offers insights into the efficacy of legal frameworks and enforcement measures. The study highlights the need for stronger national and international regulations to ensure the flow of legal trade in ODS, as well as public awareness campaigns and alternative substance development to combat illegal trade and protect the ozone layer for future generations.
