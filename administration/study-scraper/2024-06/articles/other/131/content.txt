**Paragraph 1**
Introduction
The relationship between air pollution and Land Surface Temperature (LST) is a pressing concern in the context of escalating urban heat dynamics. As the world's population continues to grow, urbanization and industrialization are leading to increased concentrations of air pollutants, which in turn exacerbate the Urban Heat Island (UHI) effect. This study aims to address this research gap by developing a spatial-temporal framework using remote sensing data from Google Earth Engine (GEE) to assess the levels of multiple air pollutants and their impact on LST in Bangladesh.

**Paragraph 2**
Methods
This study employed Emerging Hotspot Analysis, Geographically Weighted Regression (GWR), and Principal Component Analysis (PCA) to create a Pollutant Impact Index (PII). The study used remote sensing data from GEE to assess the levels of Nitrogen Dioxide (NO2), Carbon Monoxide (CO), Aerosol Optical Depth (AOD), Ozone (O3), Sulfur Dioxide (SO2), and Formaldehyde (HCHO) in Bangladesh. The PCA was used to create a PII, which was then correlated with LST values.

**Paragraph 3**
Results
The results revealed a global R-squared value of 0.61 with maximum local R-squared value of 0.68. Over 30% of the areas studied exhibit high-high clusters for air pollutant coefficients, with notably alarming levels of NO2 and O3 affecting 48.53% and 54.67% of the area, respectively. The PCA underscored the significant role of these pollutants, with the first three principal components accounting for 75% of the variance.

**Paragraph 4**
Discussion
The study's findings highlight the need for targeted environmental strategies to mitigate the impact of air pollution on urban heat dynamics and public health. The results suggest that a comprehensive approach is necessary to address the complex interplay between air pollutants, urban land use, and LST. The study demonstrates the potential of remote sensing data in capturing the spatial-temporal connection of multiple air pollutants and LST.

**Paragraph 5**
Conclusion
In conclusion, this study provides new insights into the spatial-temporal dynamics of air pollution and its impact on LST in Bangladesh. The results highlight the urgent need for targeted environmental strategies to mitigate the effects of air pollution on urban heat dynamics and public health. The study demonstrates the potential of remote sensing data in capturing the complex relationships between air pollutants, urban land use, and LST, and emphasizes the importance of incorporating these findings into urban planning and policy-making efforts.
