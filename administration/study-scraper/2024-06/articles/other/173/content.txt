**Paragraph 1**
The COVID-19 pandemic has spread globally, with devastating consequences. As of April 2024, there have been over 775 million confirmed cases and over 7 million deaths worldwide. In Chile, since the start of the pandemic, there have been over 5 million confirmed cases and over 62 thousand deaths. Apart from host and virus-related characteristics that increase the risk of COVID-19 morbidity, ambient air pollution may also be a significant risk factor for COVID-19 related hospitalizations. This study aims to investigate the association between acute changes in ambient air pollutant concentrations and COVID-19 related hospitalizations in Santiago, Chile.

**Paragraph 2**
The study population consisted of residents from the 32 comunas of the Province of Santiago, Chile, and two adjacent urban comunas. The data was collected between March 16th and August 31st, 2020. Daily hospital discharge data recorded during this period were obtained from the Chilean Statistical Institution and provided by the Ministry of Environment of Chile. Air quality data was collected from stationary ground-based air quality monitors in each of the nine sectors. The statistical analysis used a two-stage random-effects regression model for count data to relate daily variations in hospitalizations to daily variations in ambient air pollutant concentrations.

**Paragraph 3**
The results showed that there were 26,579 COVID-19 related hospitalizations during the study period, with 24,501 laboratory confirmed cases. The median age of participants was 62 years old, and 59% were males. The mean air pollution concentrations were: 0.73 ppm (0.43) for CO, 19.89 ppb (9.69) for NO2, 14.21 ppb (9.42) for O3, 29.78 µg/m3 (18.39) for PM2.5, and 69.44 µg/m (33.78) for PM10. The study found a significant association between daily increases in air pollution, especially PM2.5, and higher observed risk of hospitalization from COVID-19.

**Paragraph 4**
The findings of this study provide evidence that daily increases in air pollution, particularly PM2.5, are associated with a higher observed risk of hospitalization from COVID-19. The study also found that females and those aged 65 years or older may be disproportionately affected by these associations. The results suggest that reducing ambient air pollution levels could potentially reduce the number of COVID-19 related hospitalizations in Santiago, Chile.

**Paragraph 5**
In conclusion, this study provides new evidence on the association between acute changes in ambient air pollutant concentrations and COVID-19 related hospitalizations in Santiago, Chile. The findings have important implications for public health policy and urban planning, particularly in cities with high levels of air pollution. Further research is needed to confirm these results and to investigate the potential mechanisms underlying these associations.
