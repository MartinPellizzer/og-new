**Paragraph 1: Introduction**
Childhood exposure to air pollution has been linked to various respiratory health issues in adulthood. However, there is limited research examining the long-term effects of childhood air pollution exposure on adult bronchitic symptoms. The Southern California Children's Health Study aims to fill this knowledge gap by investigating the association between childhood air pollution exposure and self-reported adult bronchitic symptoms. This study adds to our understanding of the impact of environmental factors on respiratory health across the lifespan.

**Paragraph 2: Methods**
The study used spatial interpolation methods to estimate nitrogen dioxide (NO2), ozone, particulate matter <2.5μm (PM2.5), and <10μm (PM10) exposures based on childhood residential histories. Bronchitic symptoms were assessed through questionnaires in adulthood. Logistic regression was used to estimate associations between mean air pollution exposure across childhood and self-reported adult bronchitic symptoms, adjusting for childhood bronchitic symptoms and asthma.

**Paragraph 3: Results**
The study found that adult bronchitic symptoms were associated with NO2 and PM10 exposures during childhood. For every standard deviation increase in NO2 and PM10 exposure, the odds of reporting bronchitic symptoms increased by 1.69 and 1.51 times, respectively. These associations remained significant after adjusting for childhood respiratory health outcomes.

**Paragraph 4: Discussion**
The findings suggest that early-life air pollution exposure may have long-term effects on adult respiratory health. The associations between NO2 and PM10 exposures and bronchitic symptoms were not fully explained by childhood respiratory health impacts, indicating that other mechanisms may be at play. Furthermore, the results indicate that children with asthma may be more susceptible to the negative effects of air pollution.

**Paragraph 5: Conclusions**
In conclusion, this study provides evidence for a link between childhood NO2 and PM10 exposures and self-reported adult bronchitic symptoms. The findings support the importance of reducing air pollution exposure during critical periods of development to protect long-term respiratory health. Additionally, children with asthma may be particularly vulnerable to these effects, highlighting the need for targeted interventions to mitigate the impact of air pollution on respiratory health outcomes.
