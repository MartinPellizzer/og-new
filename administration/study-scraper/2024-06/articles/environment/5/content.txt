The Amazon rainforest is a vital component of the Earth's ecosystem, playing a crucial role in global atmospheric chemistry. Biogenic volatile organic compounds (BVOCs) released from this forest landscape interact with ozone, a ubiquitous gas in the troposphere, to form complex chemical reactions that influence local and regional air quality. The vertical distribution of BVOCs in the Amazon rainforest is essential for understanding these interactions and their impact on atmospheric chemistry.

**Paragraph 2**
To investigate the vertical distribution of BVOCs, a tower-based measurement campaign was conducted at three heights (80, 150, and 325 meters) using a proton transfer time-of-flight mass spectrometer (PTR-ToF-MS). The measurements were complemented by laboratory experiments to investigate the interference of ozone on PTR-ToF-MS signals. An ozone scrubber was used to reduce interferences, which was found to be effective for most compounds except sesquiterpenes.

The results show that isoprene and its oxidation products have a strong vertical gradient, with high concentrations near the forest canopy. The vertical distribution of carbonyl compounds also varied depending on their chemical precursors and atmospheric lifetimes.
